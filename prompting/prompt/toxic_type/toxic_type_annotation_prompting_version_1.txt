3. DEFINITION
- Offensive
    - Offensive language encompasses speech or text that is harsh, rude, disrespectful, unreasonable, profane, abusive or emotionally negative, regardless of whether it directly targets a person or group.
    - It is used
        1. To express emotions such as anger, irritation, or frustration, through profanity or exclamatory swearing that is not directed at anyone.
        2. To attack or insult an individual or a group of people in a personal or situational context, but not based on protected social attributes such as race, gender, religion, nationality, or sexuality.
    - It includes insults, threats, harassment, slurs, blaming, name-calling, and demeaning jokes that degrade or humiliate others, yet without invoking systemic discrimination or hate ideology.
    - It can manifest not only in explicit form (direct insults, abusive words, vulgar language) but also in implicit forms (sarcasm, ridicule, or superficially 'polite' remarks that convey negativity).
- Hate
    - Hate speech encompasses speech or text that is harsh, rude, disrespectful, unreasonable, profane, abusive, and is intended to attack, demean, or incite hostility toward individuals or groups based on their social identities, such as race, ethnicity, gender, religion, nationality, region, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristics.
    - It seeks to cause emotional, psychological, or social harm by reinforcing stereotypes, promoting prejudice, exclusion, or discrimination, and may threaten, ridicule, or dehumanize members of targeted communities.
    - Hate language includes, but is not limited to, insults, slurs, threats, harassment, name-calling, mocking, demeaning jokes, and language that perpetuates hate ideologies or normalizes systemic discrimination.
    - Some criteria to identify hate speech:
        1. Attacking specific groups based on social attributes.
        2. Inciting others to hate, marginalize, or discriminate against minorities.
        3. Creating prejudice, rejection, or disgust for minorities based on stereotypes and distorted facts.
        4. Using sarcasm, irony or humor to ridicule groups, even when the authorâ€™s intent is ambiguous or seemingly non-malicious.
